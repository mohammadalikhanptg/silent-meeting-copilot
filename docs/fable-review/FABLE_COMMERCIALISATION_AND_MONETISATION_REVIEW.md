# FABLE COMMERCIALISATION AND MONETISATION REVIEW
Silent Meeting Copilot (SMC). Independent Fable 5 review, 6 July 2026.
Market data points cited from research performed 6 July 2026 (see master report section 9).

## 1. Commercial viability

Viable, conditionally. The condition is repositioning. As "a live meeting coach," SMC enters a market where Hedy offers real-time coaching from free with SOC 2 and DPAs, Otter bundles live coaching into an $8 to $17 per user per month note-taker, and Fireflies bundles Live Assist into $10 to $39 tiers. A solo-built product cannot win that fight on features or price, and has no distribution to win it on reach.

As "interview integrity and live interviewer coaching for recruiters," SMC enters a niche with real pain (AI-assisted candidate cheating, CV embellishment, defensibility of hiring decisions), a budget anchor closer to screening/background-check spend than note-taker spend, and a flagship capability (cited evidence packs) no mainstream tool matches in kind. Viability there is credible but unproven; proving it is the entire purpose of the design-partner phase.

## 2. Market, ICP, urgency, budget holder

- Market category to claim: interview intelligence / interview integrity, deliberately not "AI meeting assistant" (owned by incumbents) and not "conversation intelligence" (owned by Gong-class enterprise sales tools).
- ICP for V1: UK recruitment agencies, 2 to 50 recruiters, doing screening and client-submission interviews on Zoom/Teams/Meet; secondary: in-house TA teams at mid-size firms. Rationale: agency economics make a bad candidate costly, decision cycles are short, and the buyer is often the user.
- Buyer urgency: MEDIUM-HIGH and rising. Candidate-side AI cheating is a live, publicised anxiety in recruitment. External research required to quantify (survey data, industry press), but the design-partner conversations will surface it directly.
- Budget holder: agency owner/director; in-house, Head of TA. Note the dossier's own observation that customer-service later means a different buyer (ops/IT/security); that confirms deferring that vertical.

## 3. Pricing model and packaging

The dossier's model: base tiers on billable processed meeting minutes, stackable add-ons, possible 3-month minimum on interviewer with 7 to 14-day trial. Assessment:

- Pure metered minutes as the headline is wrong for this market. Every comparable tool prices per seat per month; buyers budget per head. Metered-only pricing creates bill anxiety, complicates procurement, and prices against cost rather than value. Keep minutes as a fair-use/overage mechanism, not the headline.
- Per-interview value metric fits the Interviewer add-on far better than minutes. Recruiters count interviews and placements. An interview verification pack has a unit value a minute does not.
- Recommended structure (provisional, to be tested with design partners):
  - Coach (base): per seat per month, includes N processed hours per month, overage per hour beyond. Anchor above note-takers, below sales CI: indicative GBP 29 to 39 per seat per month. Justification: it does more than a note-taker and must not look like one on price.
  - Interviewer add-on: per seat uplift plus included interviews, indicative GBP 40 to 60 per seat per month or a per-interview price in the GBP 5 to 10 range at volume. Anchor against screening spend, not transcription spend.
  - Enterprise: bespoke, later.
- Minimum term: drop the 3-month minimum for design partners (kills trial willingness); revisit as billing policy at self-serve, as the dossier already intends (billing policy, not runtime state, is the right implementation).
- All numbers above are hypotheses to test in the first ten conversations, not decisions. The dossier is correct that prices are UNKNOWN; the correction is that they must be learned from buyers, not derived internally.
- Gate: pricing cannot be signed off until coach-path unit economics are instrumented (issue A9). If Opus cost per processed hour is high, tiering and the summary-model mix must absorb it before margins are promised.

## 4. Trial, demo, sales motion

- Sales-led first, self-serve later. The product needs a helper install, consent framing, and trust questions answered; that is a guided motion for the first 20 to 50 customers. Self-serve Stripe is a Phase 4 output of learning, not a prerequisite.
- Demo strategy: a 15-minute live demo on a staged interview is the whole pitch; the evidence pack at the end closes it. Build one canned demo interview (script plus reference CV) as a reusable asset.
- Trial: 14 days or 5 interviews, whichever first, manually provisioned during the design-partner phase.
- First commercial offer (recommended): "Founding agency programme." 5 to 10 agencies, 3-month engagement at a founding price (for example 50 percent of target), manual onboarding, weekly feedback call, case-study rights. Invoiced via Xero. This converts the missing billing system from a blocker into a non-issue.

## 5. Support and onboarding model

Undefined today (dossier concedes). Minimum for design partners: founder-led onboarding call, email support with a stated next-business-day response, an install guide, an FAQ, and the monitoring from issue A4 so failures are seen before the customer reports them. Formal SLAs deferred to self-serve.

## 6. Compliance and trust requirements (commercial view)

These are sales blockers, not just legal hygiene:
- DPA plus sub-processor list: first question from any agency handling candidate data. Blocking.
- Recording-consent guidance per jurisdiction: agencies will ask "is this legal to use"; SMC must answer with documentation, while the obligation remains the customer's. Blocking (documentation, not certification).
- Employment-AI position (EU AI Act class, Article 22 posture): blocking for the Interviewer add-on specifically. SMC's human-in-the-loop cited-evidence design is a genuine advantage here; once papered, market it.
- SOC 2: not blocking for SMB agencies, but Hedy advertising SOC 2 Type I sets the comparison. Put SOC 2 on the post-revenue roadmap; publish the security page now as the interim trust asset.

## 7. Objections, threats, differentiation

Expected sales objections and answers to prepare:
1. "Otter/Fireflies already does live help and costs less." Answer: they assist generically; SMC verifies claims live and produces a cited, defensible evidence pack; bot-free and platform-agnostic.
2. "Is coaching the interviewer fair to candidates?" Answer: ethics-locked design, human decision retained, transparency text, evidence-based rather than gut-based; arguably fairer than status quo.
3. "Is this legal (GDPR, AI rules)?" Answer: the trust pack plus the counsel-backed position. Without those, this objection ends the deal.
4. "Candidates will object to recording." Answer: consent guidance plus the compliance acknowledgement flow already built.
5. "What happens when it goes down mid-interview?" Answer: monitoring, fallback behaviour (transcript continues even if coaching degrades), support commitment.
6. "Who are you?" Solo-vendor risk. Answer: PTG corporate wrapper, security page depth, founding-programme framing, and named references after the first partners.

Competitive threats: incumbents extending interview features (Otter already advertises real-time interview help and candidate insight; Fireflies advertises candidate scoring). Their exposure is the inverse of SMC's: quick features without the evidence/compliance architecture. SMC's window is to own the integrity/defensibility position before an incumbent papers it. That window is why speed to first customers matters more than platform completeness.

## 8. Monetisation risks

- Negative unit margin if Opus cost per minute is unmeasured (A9).
- Metering disputes if billable-minute definition is fuzzy (define processed versus connected versus silent-gated minutes precisely before any invoice references minutes).
- Churn from helper friction (unsigned installer, pairing bugs) before value is felt.
- Regulatory stop on the highest-value add-on if A3 goes badly; mitigation is doing it early.
- Single-founder sales bandwidth: the motion above is deliberately sized to 5 to 10 partners for this reason.

## 9. Revenue assumptions and what must be proven before monetisation scales

Assumptions to validate (none currently evidenced):
1. Recruiters will change in-interview behaviour to use a coach (activation).
2. The evidence pack is valued enough to pay per interview or a seat uplift (willingness to pay).
3. Weekly active interviewing per seat is high enough for per-seat pricing to hold (usage intensity).
4. Trust pack answers are sufficient for SMB agency procurement (deal velocity).

Proof bar before building full self-serve monetisation: at least 3 design partners converting to paid at or near target pricing, with at least one publicly referenceable, and unit economics showing gross margin above 70 percent at target price. Until then, Stripe work is speculative.

## 10. Suggested pricing experiments

1. Founding programme at 50 percent of target seat price; measure conversion to full price at month 3.
2. A/B the Interviewer metric in conversations only (per-interview versus seat uplift); pick by which the buyer repeats back naturally.
3. Overage tolerance: give design partners uncapped minutes, measure real usage distribution, set included-hours tiers from data.
4. Willingness-to-pay ladder in the first ten conversations: present GBP 29/49/79 seat anchors and record reactions before quoting the real number.
